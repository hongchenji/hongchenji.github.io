Diffusion Policy: Visuomotor Policy Learning via Action Diffusion

March 4, 2026 · map[name:Hongchen Ji]

Paper Information#

Authors
Cheng Chi, Zhenjia Xu, Siyuan Feng, Eric Cousineau, Yilun Du,
Benjamin Burchfiel, Russ Tedrake, Shuran Song

Conference
Robotics: Science and Systems (RSS)

Year
2023

Paper
https://diffusion-policy.cs.columbia.edu/diffusion_policy_2023.pdf

1. Background and Problem#

Research area

Robot learning / visuomotor policy learning.

Core problem

Learning policies that map observations (e.g., images and robot states) to robot actions.

This is challenging because:

  • The action distribution can be multi-modal (multiple valid ways to complete a task).
  • Actions across time are sequentially correlated.
  • Manipulation tasks often require high precision and stable control.

Limitations of previous approaches

Previous policy learning methods often struggle with:

  • Representing multi-modal action distributions
  • Capturing long-horizon temporal dependencies
  • Producing stable actions in continuous control tasks

2. Core Idea#

The key idea of Diffusion Policy is to model the policy as a conditional diffusion process over robot actions.

Instead of generating images (as in standard diffusion models), the model generates robot action sequences.

Formally, the diffusion model learns the conditional distribution:

p(A_t | O_t)

where:

  • O_t : observation history
  • A_t : future action sequence

The denoising process is conditioned on the observation input.

3. Model Architecture#

At time step t:

  • The policy receives the most recent Tₒ observations.
  • The model predicts Tₚ future actions.
  • Only the first Tₐ actions are executed before replanning.

This creates a receding-horizon control loop.

The diffusion model is trained to approximate:

p(A_t | O_t)

rather than the joint distribution p(A_t, O_t), which simplifies inference.

4. Key Advantages#

Compared with traditional policy learning approaches:

  • Naturally models multi-modal action distributions
  • Produces smooth action sequences
  • Handles long-horizon manipulation tasks
  • Improves robustness compared with direct regression policies

5. Experiments#

The authors evaluate Diffusion Policy on several manipulation tasks, including:

  • Push-T
  • Franka Kitchen
  • Robomimic benchmarks

Results show that diffusion policies outperform:

  • Behavioral Cloning
  • Transformer policies
  • Other imitation learning baselines

particularly on complex manipulation tasks.

6. Main Contributions#

The paper introduces:

  1. A diffusion-based formulation for robot policies
  2. A method for generating multi-step action sequences
  3. Strong performance on multiple robot manipulation benchmarks

7. Relevance to Robotics#

Diffusion Policy is important because it shows that generative models can be used to represent robot policies.

This approach is particularly useful for:

  • multi-modal manipulation
  • complex sequential actions
  • imitation learning from demonstrations

8. Personal Notes#

Potential insights:

  • Diffusion models provide a flexible way to represent complex action distributions.
  • Predicting action sequences instead of single actions helps stabilize control.

Possible limitations:

  • Diffusion sampling may introduce latency in real-time control.
  • Training may require large datasets.

Questions to explore further:

  • How well does diffusion policy scale to long-horizon tasks?
  • Can diffusion policies be integrated with VLA models?
